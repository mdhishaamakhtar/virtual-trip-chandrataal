For Vaniya

A small trip north

You said life has felt monotonous, and four years is a long time to not see the mountains. I cannot take you there from Bangalore right now, so I made you this.

For the low days, for the gap year, for the poems you keep carrying, and for the Everest you will climb one day.

Chapter I

The city that held you

A city can let you leave and still travel inside your bag.

Lucknow, before departure

There is an hour in Lucknow when the light turns into cooling brass. It sits on old walls, on scooter mirrors, on the wet lip of a tea glass. You have lived inside that light for years. It has watched you hurry, wait, study, doubt yourself, become sure again, and carry a hundred quiet plans without showing them to everyone.

That morning, leaving did not feel like a brave thing. It felt small and practical. One bag. One ticket. A shawl folded twice. A bottle of water. Your phone at eighty percent. The name Chandra Taal had lived in your head for so long that it no longer sounded like a place. It sounded like a promise you had made to yourself in private and now had to honor.

You moved through the house carefully, as if noise could change your mind. A cup was rinsed and placed upside down. A charger was checked again. A message was typed, erased, and typed again with fewer words. This was not running away. It was quieter than that. It was the moment a person finally stops negotiating with a dream and simply begins to pack for it.

On the way to Charbagh, the city kept offering you reasons to stay. The smell of jasmine near a crossing. A handcart piled with guavas. A boy running with school shoes in one hand. The azaan rising from different directions and meeting somewhere above the road. You looked at all of it carefully, not because you were leaving forever, but because leaving changes how love looks. Suddenly every ordinary thing becomes evidence.

At the station, people were carrying steel tiffins, suitcases, sleeping children, entire versions of home. You stood among them with your bag near your feet and felt two things at once: the ache of going, and the strange lightness of not asking anyone for permission. The train had not arrived yet, but some part of you had already stepped north.

You thought of all the women you had been in this city. The careful one. The funny one. The ambitious one. The one who said yes too quickly, the one who stayed quiet too long, the one who kept a private map in her head when the room became too small. They were all coming with you. None of them had to be abandoned at the platform.

When the train finally pulled in, the platform changed mood at once. Bags rose. Bodies leaned forward. The future became a metal doorway with a number painted beside it. You climbed in, found your seat, and looked out at Lucknow through glass. The city did not disappear. It became the first page.

Chapter II

The train that carried night

Night does not pass on a train. It keeps changing seats.

Train north, 21:40

The train left Lucknow with the slow confidence of something that had done this forever. Your window did not close all the way. At first the air through the gap was only air, warm and dusty. Then the city thinned. The shops became warehouses, the warehouses became fields, and the fields became a darkness large enough to hold your thoughts without answering them.

A woman across from you fell asleep before the platform ended. Someone opened a packet of chips. A child asked the same question until his father laughed. You watched faces appear and disappear in the glass, your own among them, floating over the black outside. You were still yourself, but in the window you looked slightly unfamiliar, as if the journey had already started editing you.

For the first hour, you kept checking your phone. Messages arrived, then stopped. The network weakened. The little symbols in the corner of the screen became less certain. It should have made you anxious. Instead, it felt like a curtain being drawn, not to shut you out, but to give you privacy.

Sleep came in pieces. Every time you woke, the world had changed. Moonlit sugarcane. A station with one yellow bulb. A river flashing silver for three seconds and then gone. Distance did not feel like adventure yet. It felt like being carefully unstitched from the familiar, thread by thread, while everyone else slept through the work.

At some point, the train slowed near a town you would never know the name of. A man on the platform poured tea from a height, amber liquid catching the light before it fell into small cups. You almost got down. You did not. The train moved again, and the tea stall slid backward into the dark like a memory that belonged to somebody else.

By three in the morning, the air had teeth. You wrapped the shawl tighter and stopped pretending to read. Somewhere beyond the window, the north was gathering itself. You could feel it before you could see it, a coldness in the dark, a change in the rhythm of the tracks, a quiet warning that the easy part of the map was ending.

You were not fearless. That mattered. Fear was there in the berth with you, small and awake, but it was not in charge. Beside it was another feeling, harder to name: the sense that you were moving toward a version of yourself who had been waiting patiently, without complaint, for you to arrive.

Chapter III

What the Beas already knew

The river speaks first, before the mountains show their faces.

Manali, first altitude

In Manali, you heard the Beas before you saw it. The sound reached you between buildings, rough and bright, water striking stone with complete belief in itself. You followed it almost without deciding to. After the train and the bus and the tiredness of changing seats, the river felt like a voice that did not need to impress anyone.

You stood on a bridge and let the noise fill the parts of you that were still carrying Lucknow. The river was not calm, but it made you calmer. It had come from somewhere higher and was going somewhere lower. It did not pause to explain itself. There was comfort in that, in watching something move with purpose and no apology.

The mountains did not arrive like scenery. They arrived as pressure, in your ears, in your lungs, in the way every road seemed to lift under your feet. Deodars stood straight and dark, like the hillside had learned how to pray upward. You kept looking up and then laughing quietly at yourself, because there was always more up.

In the bazaar, a woman sold you dried apricots from a paper packet. She spoke quickly, smiling before you understood the sentence, and you smiled back because warmth is sometimes a language of its own. Your fingers smelled sweet for hours. You kept lifting them near your face without thinking, carrying that small fruit-sugar through lanes of wool caps, rain jackets, steam, and mud.

The day was full of practical tasks: finding the next shared ride, buying socks, asking twice about the road, pretending you understood the difference between confidence and luck. But underneath all of it was a quieter change. You were beginning to listen to your body. When it asked for food, you fed it. When it asked for rest, you stopped. This too felt like travel, learning not to treat yourself as an obstacle.

That night, the guesthouse room had four blankets and a window facing trees. You used all four. The river kept talking below, softer now but still awake. For the first time on the trip, nobody near you knew who you were supposed to be. Nobody expected an answer, a mood, a version of you they recognized. You were tired, cold, and free in a way that felt almost too simple to trust.

Before sleeping, you wrote one sentence in your journal: I am farther than yesterday. It looked plain on the page. It did not say enough. Still, when you read it back, you felt something settle. Farther was not only distance. Farther was proof.

Chapter IV

The pass at the top of everything

Above the trees, the world stops pretending to be gentle.

Rohtang Pass, 3978 metres

The jeep left before sunrise. Manali was still half asleep, shutters down, tea stalls beginning to glow. You sat by the window and watched the road climb out of the valley in tight bends. With every turn, the trees changed. With every turn, the air asked a little more from your chest.

At first everyone in the jeep tried to talk. Someone asked where you were from. Someone compared routes. Someone claimed the road was better this year, then immediately pointed at a broken edge where the mountain had fallen away. After a while, conversation thinned. The road became too serious for small talk.

By the time light came, clouds were below the road. That was the first real shock. Not snow, not height, but the feeling that the world had quietly rearranged its order while you were not looking. The soft parts had been left behind. The land became rock, wind, old ice, and a sky so near it felt like it could press a thumbprint into your forehead.

Rohtang did not welcome you, and that was part of its truth. The wind came from three directions at once. Your eyes watered immediately. Your hands forgot how to be warm. Two passengers stepped out, laughed nervously, and went back in. You stayed because some stubborn part of you wanted to know what would happen if you did not retreat the moment the place became difficult.

Nothing dramatic happened. No revelation opened in the clouds. You simply stood there, breathing badly, looking at snowfields that had no interest in being understood. And still, something loosened. Not joy. Relief, maybe. The relief of being somewhere that did not flatter you, did not soften itself for you, and still allowed you to stand inside it.

On the descent, the landscape changed color. Green withdrew. Brown and silver took over. Villages appeared where villages seemed impossible, a few roofs, a few walls, smoke rising thinly into huge air. You thought about the courage of ordinary life in a place like this. Not the dramatic kind people praise from far away, but the daily kind: waking, cooking, repairing, waiting out weather, beginning again.

By evening, your face felt windburned and your hair smelled of dust. You were more tired than you wanted to admit. But under the tiredness was a clean feeling, almost bright. You had crossed something. The map would call it a pass. Your body knew it as a threshold.

Chapter V

The silence of Key

Some places answer only after you stop performing a question.

Spiti, thin blue air

Spiti changed the scale of everything. The valley was not lush, not soft, not generous in the usual ways. It was ochre, ash, walnut, stone, and a blue sky so severe it felt newly invented. Villages held to the slopes by stubbornness. Prayer flags wore themselves into thread and kept moving anyway.

You had imagined silence as emptiness. Spiti corrected you. Its silence was full of small things: wind pressing through a crack, the dry scrape of your shoe on dust, your own breathing becoming louder than you expected. Without city noise, your thoughts had nowhere to hide. Some came gently. Some arrived like people you had been avoiding.

The road moved beside the river for long stretches, then rose away from it, then returned. The water looked cold enough to be a warning. Every few kilometres, the valley opened wider and made your private worries look almost shy. You did not feel unimportant in a cruel way. You felt relieved of the need to be the center of every question.

Key Monastery appeared around a bend like a sentence you had been trying to finish. White walls climbed the hill in layers. Dark windows watched the valley. Somewhere inside, a drum sounded once, then again, and the space around it grew larger. You sat below the monastery with your journal open and wrote nothing. It was the most honest thing you could have done.

A monk walked past, looked where you were looking, and nodded once. You nodded back. No greeting, no explanation. It was enough. You had come a long way to be quiet, but the quiet was not empty. It was holding you without asking you to become impressive inside it.

Later, you walked slowly because the air made speed feel foolish. Your heart beat harder on small slopes. Your lips were dry. Your hands were cold even in sunlight. The body became honest at altitude. It did not care how composed you looked. It asked for patience, and for once, you gave it some.

That evening, the sky changed in layers: blue, silver, bruised violet. You watched until the monastery lost its edges and became a pale shape on the hill. You had thought the journey would make you feel powerful. Instead, it made you feel clear. That was better.

Chapter VI

Moon lake

At the end, the map becomes water.

Chandra Taal, 4250 metres

The last walk was not cinematic. It was not the kind of moment that announces itself with music. It was stones under your shoes, wind against your face, breath counting itself, and the small negotiation between your body and the altitude. You kept walking because the map said the lake was close, and because by then you had learned that close in the mountains is not a promise. It is only encouragement.

The path kept disappearing into earth that looked the same in every direction. You stopped more than once to check whether you were still moving toward anything real. The mountains did not help. They stood around you with their ancient patience, giving no sign, offering no reassurance. You had to continue without being comforted by certainty.

Then Chandra Taal appeared without ceremony. One moment, bare mountains and tired legs. The next, water. Blue-green, crescent-shaped, impossible, held in a bowl of brown earth as if the sky had spilled and decided to stay. You stopped so suddenly that your own silence startled you.

You sat because your legs chose before your mind could make a decision. The lake was so still that reflection stopped being reflection. It became another world arranged beneath this one, a second sky, a second range of mountains, perfect and unreachable. For a while, you did not try to make meaning from it. You just let it be larger than you.

There was no one to perform the moment for. No one to say the right sentence to. No one to watch your face and decide what it meant. The privacy of that felt enormous. You had imagined arrival as a rush, but it was quieter than that. It was a deep settling, like some long-held breath had finally found a place to end.

And then, slowly, Lucknow returned. Not as homesickness. As presence. The people you love, the language you think in, the rooms that formed you, the younger version of you who once looked at this place on a screen and wondered if she would ever be brave enough to go. None of it had been left behind. It had traveled in you, quietly, all the way to the edge of the moon lake.

The light changed. The mountains turned gold, then rose, then a violet that felt almost unreal. Cold came quickly, the way it does at altitude, all at once and without negotiation. You stayed a little longer anyway. Some moments ask to be witnessed completely. This one had waited for you.

You came all this way. The lake kept the proof.

Back to Lucknow

Keep a little mountain

Now you have to come back, to the routine, to the page, to work, to the days that sometimes repeat themselves. I hope this made the room feel a little wider for a while.

The real mountains are still waiting. Everest too, somewhere ahead of you. Until you stand there yourself, keep this as a small proof that the road is still open.
